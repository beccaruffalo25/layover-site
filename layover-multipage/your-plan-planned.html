Planning Essay

Your Plan, Planned

One of the questions I'm asked most often after a trip is, how did you even find that? There isn't a single answer. I genuinely love the search; the late-night digging, comparing, clicking through maps and menus until something finally feels right. Over time, I've built a collection of free tools that help me find the best prices and the most interesting experiences — and I'm sharing them here.

It's also worth noting that my travel style lives somewhere between backpacking and luxury. I don't aim for either extreme. My goal is always the best experience for the money spent, while staying safe, comfortable, and unhurried.

One important caveat: These tools aren't always the fastest way to plan a trip. Using them well takes time, curiosity, and a willingness to dig. If you enjoy the research process, they can be incredibly rewarding. If you'd rather skip that step, the pre-planned itineraries on this site offer the same approach, just with the work already done.

Flights

Skyscanner

My starting point for almost every trip. Best for comparing routes across airlines and surfacing cheaper or more flexible options I wouldn't have thought to search manually. My favorite feature: put in your airport, round trip, your travel dates, and select "everywhere" — it shows you the most affordable destinations for that window.

Google Flights

Once I have a general sense of pricing, I switch to Google Flights. Excellent for tracking price changes, understanding what's "normal" for a route, and quickly comparing day-by-day costs. If I'm timing a booking, this is the tool I trust most.

Stays

Expedia

Reliable, straightforward, and good for hotels. I like it when I want flexibility, clear cancellation policies, or to bundle stays. My rule: any place with a review score above a 7 with 100+ reviews will probably be a decent stay.

Hotwire

For deals with intention. I use it when I'm comfortable trading exact hotel names for lower prices. The site typically gives you 3 potential hotels and reveals which one after you book — great if you can research via reverse image search.

Airbnb

Best for longer stays or when I want to feel settled in a neighborhood. I prioritize walkability, natural light, and realistic reviews over novelty. Not always the cheapest, but it depends on destination and time of year.

Adventures

Get Your Guide

My go-to for booking experiences once I know where I'm going — boat tours, food tours, day trips, skip-the-line tickets. The reviews are reliable, and it's great for locking in plans ahead of time without committing to a full tour-style vacation.

TikTok

A great source for out-of-the-box adventure ideas, food recommendations, and "is it worth it" reviews. Itineraries and inspiration guides can help you find unique adventures and decide if they're truly worth it.

Pinterest

The perfect source for visual planning and trip flow. I use it to save scenic spots, aesthetic cafés, outfit inspo, and loose day-by-day ideas. Less about exact logistics, more about shaping the vibe.

Logistics

Omio (Europe Specific)

My favorite tool for getting from point A to point B in Europe. Compares flights, trains, buses, and ferries in one place — easy to choose the fastest, cheapest, or most scenic option.

Expedia (Rental Cars)

For rental cars, Expedia makes it easy to compare across companies quickly with clear total pricing and easy filtering by pickup location, transmission type, and insurance options.

ChatGPT

I use ChatGPT to connect all the dots — turn scattered ideas into a realistic itinerary, estimate travel times, sanity-check routes, and optimize day-by-day plans. Especially useful for "does this order make sense?" or "am I trying to do too much in one day?"
